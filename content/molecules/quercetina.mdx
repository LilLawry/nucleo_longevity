claim: "A flavonoid with senolytic action, often combined with dasatinib."
bottomLine: "A genuinely interesting idea — clearing 'zombie' senescent cells — but the human evidence is early, tiny, and mostly tied to the prescription drug dasatinib, not quercetin alone."
evidenceSummary: "Quercetin is the best-known 'natural' half of the senolytic pairing dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q), the combination that produced the first human senolytic pilot data. The concept is compelling: selectively push senescent cells into apoptosis to reduce their inflammatory output. But the human trials so far are very small, use the drug combination rather than quercetin by itself, and target specific diseases (e.g. pulmonary fibrosis, diabetic kidney disease) rather than healthy aging. On its own, quercetin's bioavailability is poor. Grade C reflects a promising but immature field."
mechanism: "Acts as a senolytic by interfering with pro-survival pathways that senescent cells depend on, tipping them toward apoptosis. Also has general antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activity as a dietary flavonoid — which is a different, weaker claim than true senolysis."
safety: "Well tolerated as a normal dietary flavonoid (onions, apples, capers, tea). The senolytic *protocols* are a different matter: D+Q involves a prescription chemotherapy agent (dasatinib) and belongs strictly in a clinical/trial setting, not in self-experimentation."
dosageContext: "As a supplement, quercetin has low oral bioavailability, and doses/cycles vary widely. In senolytic research it is dosed intermittently ('hit-and-run') alongside dasatinib — a regimen that should not be replicated outside a trial."
