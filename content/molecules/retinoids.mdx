evidenceSummary: "Topical retinoids are the most studied 'anti-aging' actives after photoprotection: they improve the appearance of fine lines, texture and tone and increase dermal collagen in controlled studies. The nuance is potency — prescription retinoids (e.g. tretinoin) have the strongest data but are regulated medicines; cosmetic retinol and retinaldehyde are milder and convert to the active form in skin, so effects are real but slower. Grade B reflects solid cosmetic evidence for the over-the-counter forms. The ingredient's evidence is not a promise for any specific product: concentration, stability and formulation decide the result."
mechanism: "Bind retinoic-acid receptors to modulate keratinocyte turnover and stimulate fibroblast collagen synthesis while reducing collagen-degrading MMPs; retinol/retinaldehyde are converted to retinoic acid in the skin."
safety: "Commonly cause dryness, redness and peeling ('retinization'), especially early; increase sun sensitivity, so pairing with daily SPF matters. Avoid in pregnancy without medical advice. Introduce slowly. This is cosmetic use — prescription retinoids for medical conditions are a doctor's domain."
dosageContext: "Cosmetic products span roughly 0.1–1% retinol; higher isn't automatically better and often just more irritating. Consistency and tolerance-building matter more than chasing the highest percentage."
